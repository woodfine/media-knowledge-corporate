**Catholic social teaching** comprises the body of doctrine developed by the Catholic Church
on questions of social, political, and economic life. On the question of property, Catholic
social teaching occupies a distinctive position: it affirms the natural right of individuals
to own private property while simultaneously insisting that the right is bounded by the social
function of property — the obligation to use it in ways that serve the common good. This
position has generated a tradition of economic thought that engages productively with both
liberal political economy and socialist critique without fully adopting either.

## Rerum Novarum and the natural right to property

The foundational text of modern Catholic social teaching is *Rerum Novarum* (1891), the
encyclical of Pope Leo XIII issued in response to the social question generated by industrial
capitalism.[^1] Leo argued against socialist proposals to abolish private property on the
grounds that the right to own property is natural — derived from the human person's capacity
for rational foresight and the need to provide for oneself and one's family across time.
Property provides security and the basis for productive agency that wage-labour alone cannot
provide.

Leo simultaneously condemned the conditions of industrial wage-labour, criticising the
exploitation of workers, the payment of wages below subsistence, and the destruction of
traditional economic structures that had previously provided workers with a degree of economic
independence. His response was not to endorse either laissez-faire capitalism or socialism but
to call for a restoration of conditions in which workers could own property and exercise
economic agency — a vision that subsequently influenced the distributist movement and, in
different ways, Christian democratic political parties.

## Quadragesimo Anno and subsidiarity

Pius XI's *Quadragesimo Anno* (1931) extended Leo's analysis in two significant directions.[^2]
First, it articulated the principle of subsidiarity — that decisions should be made at the
lowest competent level of social organisation, and that higher-level institutions should exist
to support rather than supplant lower-level ones. Subsidiarity has become one of the most
influential concepts exported from Catholic social thought, entering political discourse across
ideological traditions and serving as a constitutional principle of the European Union.

Second, *Quadragesimo Anno* criticised the concentration of economic power in both private
monopolies and the state. Pius warned that the concentration of capital in the hands of a few
had produced an economic dictatorship that was incompatible with genuine market competition
and social solidarity alike. His call for the reconstruction of vocational guilds and the
reorganisation of economic life along associational lines anticipated elements of both
corporatist and cooperative economic thought.

## The social function of property

Running through Catholic social teaching is the claim that private ownership does not entail
unconditional rights of use. Thomas Aquinas had argued that while private ownership of
property was licit and useful for social order, the duty of charity required using one's
surplus to benefit others. Modern Catholic social teaching developed this into the doctrine
of the social function of property: the right to private ownership is real but is bounded by
an obligation to use property in ways that contribute to the common good rather than simply
serving private interest.

This doctrine has practical implications for how Catholic social thought approaches land
tenure, housing, and productive enterprise. Large accumulations of unproductive land, monopoly
rents, and financial speculation that produces no real economic value are all criticised within
this framework as inconsistent with property's social function.

## Centesimus Annus and the market economy

The collapse of Eastern European communism prompted Pope John Paul II's *Centesimus Annus*
(1991), which offered the most sustained Catholic engagement with market economics of any
modern papal document.[^3] John Paul affirmed that the market is an efficient mechanism for
allocating resources and rewarding productive activity, and that the events of 1989 had
demonstrated the failure of centrally planned alternatives. At the same time, he insisted that
markets must be circumscribed by a juridical and ethical framework — law, custom, and moral
culture — that prevents them from excluding those without economic power from participation.

*Centesimus Annus* explicitly endorsed the concept of the preferential option for the poor —
the moral priority given to the interests of those least able to protect themselves in economic
competition — and argued that a genuinely free economy was one structured so that this option
was institutionally embedded rather than dependent solely on individual charity.
