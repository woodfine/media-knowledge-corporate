The **cooperative economic model** is a framework of enterprise organisation in which the
business is owned and democratically controlled by its members — who may be workers, consumers,
producers, or some combination of these — rather than by external investors. Cooperatives
operate on a distinct set of values and principles that distinguish them from investor-owned
firms: member ownership, democratic governance, autonomy, education, cooperation among
cooperatives, and concern for the wider community.[^1]

## Structure and member categories

Cooperatives are defined by the identity of their member-owners. The primary categories are:

**Worker cooperatives** are owned and controlled by the people who work in the enterprise.
Workers hold membership interests that entitle them to vote in governance and to receive a
share of the surplus — the cooperative equivalent of profit. The Mondragón network in the
Basque Country is the most studied example of a large-scale worker cooperative system operating
in capital-intensive manufacturing and service sectors.

**Consumer cooperatives** are owned by their customers. Members purchase goods or services
from the cooperative and participate in its governance. Agricultural credit cooperatives,
housing cooperatives, and retail food cooperatives are common consumer cooperative forms.

**Producer cooperatives** pool the productive capacity of independent producers — typically
agricultural producers — to achieve economies of scale in processing, marketing, and
distribution while preserving individual producer ownership of the underlying assets.

**Multi-stakeholder cooperatives** incorporate more than one class of member, giving ownership
and governance rights to different stakeholder groups simultaneously. This model is growing in
the social economy and in platform contexts where the interests of workers and users are
closely intertwined.

## Governance principles

The International Co-operative Alliance's Statement on the Co-operative Identity (1995)
articulates seven principles that define cooperative practice globally.[^1] These principles
are:

1. Voluntary and open membership
2. Democratic member control (one member, one vote)
3. Member economic participation — members contribute equitably to and democratically control
   the capital of the cooperative, and surpluses are allocated equitably
4. Autonomy and independence — cooperatives are autonomous organisations under member control
5. Education, training, and information — cooperatives provide education to members, elected
   representatives, managers, and employees
6. Cooperation among cooperatives — cooperatives serve their members most effectively by
   working together through local, national, regional, and international structures
7. Concern for community — cooperatives work for the sustainable development of their
   communities

## Economic properties

The cooperative model has distinctive economic properties relative to the investor-owned firm.
Henry Hansmann's analysis of enterprise ownership identifies the conditions under which
cooperative forms are cost-effective: when the cost of market contracting between parties is
high, and when the parties who have low contracting costs have relatively homogeneous
interests.[^2]

Worker cooperatives perform well when workers possess firm-specific skills, when monitoring
costs within a conventional employment relationship are high, and when the workforce is
sufficiently small and cohesive for democratic governance to function efficiently. As scale
increases, the governance cost of cooperative democracy and the heterogeneity of member
interests tend to increase, which explains why large worker cooperatives are comparatively
rare outside of networked structures like Mondragón.

Consumer cooperatives are most viable when the goods or services being provided are difficult
for consumers to evaluate, when consumer loyalty is high, and when the cooperative can
accumulate sufficient capital from member equity and retained surplus.

## The cooperative sector in comparative perspective

In most developed economies, cooperatives constitute a small but significant share of economic
activity. They are particularly prevalent in agriculture (where producer cooperatives dominate
dairy, grain, and supply sectors in many countries), housing (where cooperative tenures are
common in northern Europe), financial services (where credit unions and cooperative banks
serve tens of millions of members), and retail food (where consumer cooperatives have market
shares exceeding 20 percent in some Scandinavian countries).[^3]

The cooperative form is often associated with social economy or solidarity economy discourse,
where its appeal derives not only from economic efficiency arguments but from its embodiment
of values — democracy, equity, solidarity — that proponents regard as intrinsically important
regardless of whether cooperatives match the productivity of investor-owned alternatives.
